First-Person View Drone

I started this project because I wanted to challenge myself with something I thought would be cool and growing up I was really into drones. I owned a Bebop drone and had flows a friendâ€™s DJI. While browsing on YouTube, I found a community that flies custom first-person view drones. This sparked my interest in designing and building my own drone.




The Challenge:
* Design a quadcopter drone that can successfully fly
* Allow video transfer from drone camera to goggles
* Ensure that drone receives commands from radio transmitter

The Process:
I began by spending my entire fall semester researching anything and everything regarding drones. I the initiated the new year by purchasing all the required tools and materials. Each weekend, or when I had the time, I would solder components to the flight controller, the brains of the drone. Once all the electronic components were connected, I installed the motors on the frame and soldered the wires to the electronic speed controller(ESC) which is what tell the motors when and how to spin.

After all the components were connected, I conducted a power test to see if everything worked. For safety reasons, I used a smoke stopper to determine if my electronics were properly wired. The greenlight was the indicator that everything was working fine. I then configured the flight controller using BetaFlight and configured the ESC to ensure each motor spun in the proper direction. This is important to generate lift.

Once everything was in order, I paired my drone with my radio transmitter and set the parameter for the sensitivity of my propulsion lever. After this, I installed my propellers and my drone was ready to fly!

Problems along the way:
* Learning curve was very high, but I persevered through and successfully solved any issues by researching the issues.
* Spacing of componenets was difficult. Despite this I found methods of consolidating wire paths using zipties or wire sleeves which created a look as an added feature.
* Initial flight attempts failed due to loose propellers. Once I figured out they needed to be tightened more, the drone was able to sucessfully fly.

End Result:
* The drone sucessfully flew! Video on the right is 1st successful test flight
* I didn't have the goggles yet, but after they arrived I was able to see live analog video from the drone
* The drone responded very well to the radio commands
* I ended up losing the drone in the windy hills of Dublin, CA :( , so the next iteration will come with a GPS tracker
